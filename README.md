# Mother Tongue: The People's Chant

**An Annotated Protest Playlist | 1939–2023**  
Feivel Daniel | Music and Social Protest | USF Spring 2026

---

## Overview

This repository is the annotation archive for *Mother Tongue: The People's Chant*, an annotated protest playlist documenting how Black communities around the world have named economic exclusion and racial dispossession through music across more than eight decades.

The playlist spans 40 tracks across the United States, United Kingdom, Nigeria, Jamaica, and Cameroon. Each track is paired with a written annotation connecting the song to its specific historical, economic, and political context. The project treats the racial wealth gap not as a fixed statistic but as a living condition that artists have been naming since 1939.

The playlist opens with a Black woman singing about a lynching and closes with a Black man from Milwaukee finally getting paid. That is the full distance of 84 years.

- **12¢** — Black families hold roughly 12 cents for every dollar of white family wealth (Federal Reserve SCF, 2022)

## Annotations

---

### 1939–1968: Terror and Testimony

**1. Strange Fruit — Billie Holiday (1939)**  
The song documents lynching as a routine feature of Black life in the first half of the 20th century. Racial terror was not separate from economic exclusion. It was the enforcement mechanism. Black people who accumulated wealth, organized labor, or challenged white authority were met with violence, and the threat of that violence kept the economic order intact.

**2. A Change Is Gonna Come — Sam Cooke (1964)**  
Written after Cooke was turned away from a whites-only motel in Louisiana, the song captures the specific tension of the mid-20th century Black experience: the belief that full participation in American life was both possible and imminent. The change he is waiting for is not abstract. It is economic, legal, and social access that had been deliberately withheld.

**3. Say It Loud (I'm Black and I'm Proud) — James Brown (1968)**  
Released four months after the assassination of Martin Luther King Jr., the song reframes Black identity as a source of strength rather than a deficit. Cultural pride became a political tool because the economic and legal gains of the civil rights movement had not translated into material equality. Black pride was the response to that gap.

**4. The Revolution Will Not Be Televised — Gil Scott-Heron (1970)**  
The song argued that mass media would not carry the revolution because mass media was owned by the same institutions the revolution was against. Gil Scott-Heron was describing a structural problem: the people with the most to gain from change had the least access to the platforms that shaped public opinion. The song was also a call to action. Real change would have to happen in the streets, not on the screen.

### 1970s: Diaspora and Dispossession

**5. What's Going On — Marvin Gaye (1971)**  
By 1971, King was dead, Malcolm was dead, Fred Hampton was dead, and the promised economic gains of the civil rights movement had not arrived for most Black Americans. Gaye's question was not rhetorical. It was a genuine reckoning with a movement that had been met with assassination, incarceration, and legislative stalling. The album documents the disillusionment of an entire generation.

**6. Soul Makossa — Manu Dibango (1972)**  
The first major disco hit by an African artist, the song became an anthem of Black cultural celebration in the early 1970s. After a decade of organized resistance, the people who had fought for basic rights were claiming space in popular culture and nightlife. The celebration was its own political statement. We are still here. We are not only our struggle.

**7. Pusherman — Curtis Mayfield (1972)**  
From the Superfly soundtrack, the song put the drug dealer's perspective at the center of Black popular culture for the first time. The antihero was a product of conditions. Redlining, wage exclusion, and the collapse of urban economies had closed off most legitimate paths to wealth. The pusher was not a villain in a vacuum. He was a rational actor in an irrational system.

**8. Gentleman — Fela Kuti (1973)**  
Fela documented in Lagos what Mayfield was documenting in Chicago: the internalization of colonial values as a status marker among Africans taught to see Western dress, language, and behavior as civilization. The "gentleman" of the title has adopted the colonizer's image at the cost of his own identity. Economic aspiration and cultural erasure were tied together. The colonizer had left. The colonizer's mindset stayed.

**9. Living for the City — Stevie Wonder (1973)**  
The song traces the pipeline from the rural South to the urban North and exposes the myth of the Great Migration as a path to freedom. The protagonist arrives in New York and is almost immediately imprisoned. Wonder was documenting what urban economists would later confirm: northern cities replicated southern racial hierarchies through housing segregation, wage discrimination, and a criminal justice system that specifically targeted Black men.

**10. Exodus — Bob Marley (1977)**  
Rastafari reframed the Black diaspora's relationship to Africa not as historical loss but as an ongoing political project. The "Babylon" Marley is fleeing is not only spiritual. It is the economic and political system of Western imperialism that had extracted labor and resources from Africa and the Caribbean for centuries. A band from a tiny Caribbean island moved people all over the world because the metaphor was universal. Every colonized person knew what Babylon meant.

### 1980s: Crack Capitalism

**11. The Breaks — Kurtis Blow (1980)**  
One of the first commercially successful rap records, the song catalogs the daily indignities of working-class Black life in New York at the end of the 1970s: job loss, eviction, bad luck stacked on bad luck. The city was in fiscal crisis, services had been gutted, and the people left behind were young Black and Latino New Yorkers. The song is not a protest. It is a document.

**12. When Am I Going to Make a Living — Sade (1984)**  
The song captures the frustration of a generation of Black professionals who had done everything right by the standards of respectability and still found themselves locked out. The civil rights generation fought for access to institutions. Their children got the access and found that access alone did not close the gap. The question in the title is still unanswered.

**13. 6 'N the Mornin' — Ice-T (1986)**  
The crack economy had created a parallel financial system in cities abandoned by legitimate investment. Ice-T's narrator is not glamorizing street life. He is describing it as the available option. Redlining had denied Black families the homeownership that built white middle-class wealth. The street was the substitute economy that filled the void.

**14. Don't Believe the Hype — Public Enemy (1988)**  
Public Enemy argued that the media's portrayal of Black communities was not neutral reporting but active propaganda serving the interests of those who benefited from Black economic exclusion. The hype was the story that crime was a cultural pathology rather than the predictable result of disinvestment, incarceration, and the deliberate destruction of Black wealth. Chuck D understood that the narrative was part of the system.

**15. F*** tha Police — NWA (1988)**  
The song named the relationship between policing and economic containment directly. Police presence in Black neighborhoods was not protection. It was enforcement of a boundary. The neighborhoods that were over-policed were the same ones that had been redlined, defunded, and economically isolated. The song was a response to occupation, not crime.

### 1990s: Street Life and Survival

**16. Brenda's Got a Baby — 2Pac (1991)**  
The song traces what happens when every institution fails at once: family, school, welfare, and the street. Brenda is not a cautionary tale about individual choices. She is the product of a system that had withdrawn every form of support from the neighborhood she was born into. The crack epidemic did not create her circumstances. It landed on top of circumstances that already existed.

**17. Gimme the Loot — The Notorious B.I.G. (1994)**  
The song goes inside the psychology of someone for whom the crack economy is the only economy that has ever made sense. The morality is not absent. It has been replaced by a different set of rules produced by a different set of conditions. Biggie was a product of Bed-Stuy in the late 1980s, a neighborhood that had been systematically defunded, redlined, and left to manage its own collapse.

**18. One Love — Nas (1994)**  
The prison industrial complex had removed a significant portion of the young Black male population from their communities by the mid-1990s. Nas writes letters to friends inside and the letters are mundane: gossip, updates, condolences. The normalization of incarceration is the point. Mass imprisonment had become so routine that it registered not as crisis but as fact of life.

**19. D'Evils — Jay-Z (1996)**  
The song is an interior monologue from inside the crack economy, tracing how the pursuit of money in conditions of scarcity produces a specific moral logic. Jay-Z is not romanticizing it. He is explaining it. The devils are not external forces. They are what the conditions produce in people.

**20. Sky's the Limit — The Notorious B.I.G. (1997)**  
Released after Biggie's death, the song is addressed to the generation coming up behind him. It is about possibility from within constraint: a kid from Brooklyn with no obvious path finding one. The song matters not as a rags-to-riches story but as evidence that the ceiling for Black aspiration was being pushed upward, even when the floor had not moved.

**21. Astronomy (8th Light) — Black Star (1998)**  
Mos Def and Talib Kweli were part of a movement that reclaimed Black identity from the crack era's dominant narrative. The song redefines what Blackness could sound like and mean at a moment when hip-hop's commercial success had started to flatten its political content. The neo-soul movement running parallel was making the same argument through different means.

### 2000s: Documentation

**22. Come Home With Me — Cam'ron (2002)**  
The Harlem that Cam'ron is bringing you into is not just a setting. It is the reason he is who he is. The argument is that you cannot separate a person from the geography that produced them, and that the geography was itself produced by decades of policy decisions that concentrated poverty in specific zip codes. The invitation is also an explanation.

**23. What More Can I Say — Jay-Z (2003)**  
The song is a declaration that the Black American dream is real and reachable regardless of where you start. Jay-Z's line about not having a Harvard degree but having the will to act is the argument: the institutions designed to be the path to wealth were never built for people like him, and he built an alternative path anyway.

**24. The Corner — Common (2005)**  
After 400 years of displacement and dispossession, Black Americans built deep attachment to specific blocks and neighborhoods the way other groups build attachment to homelands. The corner is not just a location. It is a community institution that formed in the absence of other institutions. Common is documenting that attachment as both cultural inheritance and survival mechanism.

**25. Georgia...Bush — Lil Wayne (2005)**  
Recorded in direct response to Hurricane Katrina, the song named what the federal government's response made plain: that the lives of poor Black people in New Orleans were not a priority. The storm was a natural disaster. The six-day delay in federal response was a policy choice. The levee failures had been flagged and defunded for years. Wayne said what the news would not.

**26. My President — Young Jeezy (2008)**  
The election of Barack Obama felt, in the moment, like the completion of the civil rights movement's unfinished project. Jeezy's verse captures that feeling without irony. The song matters as a document of that specific historical mood, one that would prove far more complicated than it appeared.

### 2010s: Legacy and Reckoning

**27. Sing About Me, I'm Dying of Thirst — Kendrick Lamar (2012)**  
Survivor's guilt is the condition of making it out of a place most people don't. The song documents what success looks like from inside Compton: not freedom, but weight. Every person Kendrick lost is carried forward. The freedom he is supposed to have achieved never quite arrives.

**28. Ojuelegba — Wizkid (2014)**  
The hustler mythology that Black American music developed over decades traveled back across the Atlantic and found resonance in Lagos. Ojuelegba is a working-class neighborhood, and Wizkid's pride in coming from there mirrors the same logic running through The Corner and Come Home With Me. The shared reference points were not coincidental. They were evidence of a common economic experience across the diaspora.

**29. Kno the Meaning — Future (2014)**  
The song is about what conditions of scarcity do to a person's value system over time. Future is not offering a critique from outside. He is describing a worldview that was formed inside specific conditions. The meaning he knows is the meaning the streets taught him, and the question underneath the song is whether any other meaning was ever made available.

**30. Mortal Man — Kendrick Lamar (2015)**  
The song ends with a recorded conversation between Kendrick and a 1994 Tupac interview, asking whether the next generation can trust an artist to lead when the traditional leaders are gone. King was killed. Malcolm was killed. The Panthers were dismantled. Nipsey would be killed four years after this album. The question of who carries the movement forward is not rhetorical.

**31. Castles — Skepta (2016)**  
The children of Caribbean and West African immigrants to Britain grew up in the same conditions their American counterparts documented in hip-hop: concentrated poverty, over-policing, and exclusion from wealth produced by the same empire that had colonized their parents' homelands. The 2011 London riots were the backdrop. The grievances were structural, not spontaneous. Skepta was documenting the same story from a different postcode.

**32. Legacy — Jay-Z (2017)**  
The song is explicitly about wealth as inheritance and the historical denial of that inheritance to Black families. Jay-Z frames his financial success not as personal achievement but as the first chapter of a generational project. The racial wealth gap is fundamentally a gap in what was passed down, and Legacy names that directly. This is what the whole playlist has been building toward.

**33. Dedication — Nipsey Hussle (2018)**  
Nipsey's career was structured around the idea that the hustler's discipline could be redirected toward legitimate wealth building. He bought back the block in Crenshaw. He opened a coworking space. The dedication was not only lyrical. It was operational. The song documents the mentality that made those projects possible, and his murder in 2019 in front of his own store made the stakes of that project impossible to ignore.

**34. Just How It Is — Young Thug (2019)**  
Young Thug's entire sonic identity was an argument about Black masculinity that the previous generation had not made publicly. The high register, the melodic delivery, the gender-ambiguous styling: all of it challenged the idea that there was one way to be a Black man from the hood. The song is not the statement. Young Thug himself is.

**35. Lonely Child — NBA YoungBoy (2019)**  
The mental health crisis running through young Black communities is not separate from economic conditions. It is produced by them. YoungBoy's music documents what it costs psychologically to grow up in poverty, lose people constantly, and have no institutional support for any of it. The loneliness is structural. It is what happens when an entire community is abandoned and then blamed for the consequences.

**36. Three Rivers — Dave (2019)**  
Dave is a British-Ugandan rapper from Streatham, and the song traces how race, class, and origin intersect differently in the UK than in the US while producing similar outcomes. The three rivers are metaphorical: the different streams of identity converging in someone shaped by both African heritage and British poverty. In a multicultural world, the specific details differ. The structure underneath them does not.

### 2020s: Present

**37. Different Meaning — Lil Durk (2020)**  
The song asks what success actually means when you have millions of dollars but are still losing the people you grew up with. Durk is describing a specific kind of trap: you escaped the financial conditions but the conditions kept taking everyone who couldn't. The money changed the meaning of everything except the loss. Escape turned out not to mean what it was supposed to mean.

**38. Watch Yo Homie — Lil Durk (2020)**  
The playlist ends where it began, with the same conditions producing the same outcomes in a new decade. The South Side of Chicago in 2020 is running on the same mathematics as Bed-Stuy in 1994 and Watts in 1988. The geography shifted. The structure did not. A hundred years of documentation and the story is still being written.

**39. Don't Kome 2 My Funeral — Young Slo-Be (2022)**  
Recorded months before he was killed at 24, the song is a pre-emptive farewell from someone who knew the streets had a claim on him he could not fully escape. The apathy in the title is not indifference. It is the rational response of someone who had watched the system fail everyone around him and had no expectation of it doing anything different. Young Slo-Be was from Stockton. He was somebody's Brenda. He was somebody's Kendrick who didn't make it.

**40. People's Favorite — Chicken P (2023)**  
Chicken P is from Milwaukee, one of the most segregated cities in the United States and one of the worst for Black economic mobility. The song is not about wealth. It is about being loved by the people you came from. Social capital is its own form of wealth, and it is the one form the racial wealth gap cannot fully measure. Billie Holiday started this record in 1939 naming what was being taken. Chicken P closes it in 2023 naming what was built anyway. The record is not finished.
